“Climate Change Indicators: Marine Heat Waves.” EPA, Environmental Protection Agency, 8 July 2024,
www.epa.gov/climate-indicators/climate-change-indicators-marine-heat-waves
Marine heatwaves are one of the most destructive phenomena for marine life. Stationary organisms who are unable to avoid the hotter temperatures suffer immensely and those who can move are severely displaced. Stationary life such as coral and marine grass provide countless habitats for marine life so when they are wiped out or removed from an area, the species that rely on them tend to die off quickly. In addition to killing marine life, increased ocean temperatures help to increase the acidity of the ocean leading to potentially harmful algae blooms who can thrive in new locations due to increased ocean temperatures. This algae blooms will also kill the local marine life leading to a decrease in food supply from the ocean and a decrease in economic gain from tourist attractions near the water.

Tandon, A. Tandon, Ayesha. “Melting Glaciers Drove ‘21% of Sea Level Rise’ over Past Two Decades.” Carbon Brief, 4 May 2021,
www.carbonbrief.org/melting-glaciers-drove-21-of-sea-level-rise-over-past-two-decades/.
This article, written by climate change journalist Ayesha Tandon in April 2021, discusses the alarming rate at which global warming is melting ice caps and contributing to rising sea levels. Between 2000 and 2019, the rate of glacier melting nearly doubled, increasing from 36 centimeters per year to 69 centimeters per year. Glaciers, which hold over 70% of the planet’s freshwater, lost an average of 267 billion tons of ice annually during this period, resulting in a total sea level rise of 14.06 millimeters. While the article is relatively recent, the information remains crucial for understanding the long-term impacts of climate change. Tandon, with her degree from the University of Exeter, provides well-supported data and credible insights. Overall, the article effectively informs readers about the urgent crisis in the Arctic and its implications for global sea levels.
